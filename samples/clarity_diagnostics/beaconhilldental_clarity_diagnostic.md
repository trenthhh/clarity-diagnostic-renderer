### Finding 1: No Review Solicitation System — The Root Cause

I noticed that Beacon Hill Dental has no visible system for asking patients to leave a Google review after their appointment. The evidence is in the numbers: 196 reviews, accumulating at roughly half a review per month historically. The most recent reviews visible on third-party listings are from October 2025 — six months ago as of this audit. That suggests a current velocity of 1–3 new reviews per month at most.

**Why this matters**

[STAT CALLOUT: ~0.5/month — Historical review velocity — Beacon Smiles Dental, 0.4 miles away, has 1,573 reviews]

Google Maps rankings are influenced by review volume, recency, and velocity — not just rating. A practice that receives reviews slowly and sporadically gets treated by Google's algorithm as less relevant than a practice generating consistent monthly review activity. At the current pace, Beacon Hill Dental will add roughly 24–36 reviews over the next two years. At industry standard pace, a competitor will add 192–360 reviews in the same window.

The rating itself isn't the problem. 4.5 stars is solid. The problem is the signal strength behind it. 196 reviews is a small sample by modern local SEO standards, and it tells the algorithm — and prospective patients — that this practice is either newer or less active than it actually is.

Beyond search ranking: the zero visible review response rate compounds the issue. Every unanswered review — even a positive one — is a missed opportunity to signal that the practice is engaged, attentive, and worth trusting.

**Here's what I'd do**

**Step 1 — Set up a review request workflow (this week):**
RecallMax, which the practice already uses, includes automated post-appointment messaging. Configure a review request to go out 24–48 hours after each completed appointment via text or email. The message should be short, personal, and include a direct link to the Google review form. Something like: "Thanks for coming in — it was great seeing you. If you have a moment, a quick Google review means a lot to us: [link]."

**Step 2 — Respond to existing reviews (this week):**
Go through the existing 196 Google reviews and begin responding — starting with the most recent. Responses don't need to be long. They do need to be specific. Reference the patient's experience, thank them by name, and let them know the team saw their feedback. Aim to respond to every review going forward within 48 hours.

**Step 3 — Set a review velocity target and track it (ongoing):**
The goal is 8–10 new reviews per month within 90 days. Check the count weekly. If the number isn't moving, the message or the delivery mechanism needs to be adjusted.

**Effort: Structural — requires setup once, then consistent execution**

[PULL QUOTE: "196 reviews is not a reflection of how good this practice is. It's a reflection of the fact that nobody has been asking."]

**Why this matters**

[STAT CALLOUT: 304 reviews — Gap to established-practice standard — A 10+ year practice should have 500+ Google reviews at minimum]

Review volume shapes two things: Google's assessment of the practice's relevance, and the prospective patient's first impression. The algorithm uses review count as a signal of engagement and activity. The prospective patient uses it as a proxy for trust — consciously or not.

This is a consequence of Finding 1, not an independent problem. The fix is the same: build the review solicitation system, run it consistently, and let the count grow month over month.

### Finding 3: GBP Listing Gaps — The Category Problem

I noticed that Beacon Hill Dental's Google Business Profile appears to be listed under a generic "Dentist" category with no confirmed service-specific secondary categories. The GBP description, as reflected on third-party listing data, reads: "Beacon Hill Dental is a Calgary based dental clinic in the northwest. We are conveniently located in the Beacon Hill shopping center, and offer services for the whole family. We are proud to be using the current 2018 Alberta Dental Fee Guide." This description is outdated (the 2026 fee guide is current) and doesn't mention Invisalign, dental implants, Guided Biofilm Therapy, or multilingual services.

[PULL QUOTE: "A practice that offers Invisalign, implants, and GBT but lists only 'Dentist' as its category is invisible on the service searches that bring the best patients."]

**Why this matters**

[STAT CALLOUT: 0 of 5 — High-value search terms ranked — Beacon Hill Dental does not appear in Maps results for "Calgary dentist," "Calgary family dentist," "Calgary cosmetic dentist," "Calgary dental implants," or "Calgary Invisalign"]

Google uses GBP categories as a signal for what searches a business should appear in. A practice listed only as "Dentist" competes in a pool of every dentist in Calgary. A practice listed as "Cosmetic Dentist," "Dental Implants Periodontist," and "Orthodontist" (in addition to "Dentist") becomes eligible for service-specific searches — which are the searches where the patient already knows what they want and is ready to book.

This is one of the highest-return-per-hour GBP fixes available. It doesn't require a marketing budget. It requires 30–60 minutes inside Google Business Profile.

**Here's what I'd do**

**GBP category update (30 minutes):**
Log into the Google Business Profile dashboard at business.google.com. Navigate to Edit Profile → Business category. The primary category should remain "Dentist." Add secondary categories that match the services offered:
- "Cosmetic Dentist" — for whitening, veneers, Invisalign searches
- "Dental Implants Periodontist" — for implant searches
- "Orthodontist" — for Invisalign and clear aligner searches (note: Google's available secondary categories in Canada may vary slightly; use the closest available match)

**GBP description update (30 minutes):**
Rewrite the description to be specific, keyword-rich, and current. A starting draft:

*"Beacon Hill Dental is a family-owned dental practice in NW Calgary, led by Dr. Alan Chang and his two children, who are also practicing dentists. With over 35 years of dental experience and deep roots in Alberta, our team provides comprehensive care including Invisalign, dental implants, cosmetic dentistry, Guided Biofilm Therapy, and emergency dental services. We speak Mandarin, Cantonese, Punjabi, Tagalog, Urdu, Hindi, and Vietnamese. Open six days a week with evening appointments Tuesday through Thursday. Accepting new patients and the Canadian Dental Care Plan."*

**GBP posts (ongoing):**
Start publishing one GBP post per week. Each post should take 5–10 minutes. Topics can mirror blog posts already being written, or highlight services, team members, or seasonal promotions. GBP posts reset in visibility after 7 days — consistent weekly posting keeps the listing active.

**Effort: Quick win**

### Finding 4: Invisible in the Maps Pack for Every High-Value Search

I noticed that Beacon Hill Dental does not appear in Google Maps pack results for any of the five highest-value local dental searches: "Calgary dentist," "Calgary family dentist," "Calgary cosmetic dentist," "Calgary dental implants," or "Calgary Invisalign." The practice likely appears for hyper-local searches tied to the Beacon Hill neighbourhood — but that scope limits new patient discovery to people who already live within walking distance.

**Why this matters**

[STAT CALLOUT: 0 of 5 — Service-specific searches ranked — Invisalign, implants, cosmetic, family, general — none in the Maps pack]

The Maps pack is the most visible real estate in a local dental search. Three practices appear. Everyone else is invisible. For searches like "Calgary Invisalign" or "Calgary dental implants" — where a patient has already decided what service they want — not appearing in the top three is equivalent to not existing in that patient's decision process.

Beacon Hill Dental offers all of these services. The gap isn't capability — it's signal. Review volume, GBP categories, and content depth are the levers Google uses to decide which practices rank. Right now, those levers are underleveraged.

**Here's what I'd do**

This finding resolves as Findings 1, 3, and the blog content system mature. The sequencing matters:

1. Fix the GBP categories (Finding 3) — immediate improvement in eligibility for service-specific searches
2. Build the review solicitation system (Finding 1) — review velocity improvement begins signaling to Google within 30–60 days of launch
3. Continue the blog cadence — service-specific content ("Dental Implants in NW Calgary," "Invisalign for Adults in Calgary") reinforces the service relevance signal
4. Add service pages if they don't already exist — each high-margin service should have its own dedicated URL with NW Calgary targeting in the copy

Maps pack rankings for competitive Calgary-wide searches will take 6–12 months to build. NW-specific searches ("NW Calgary dentist," "Sarcee Trail NW dentist") should move faster — within 90 days of implementing the GBP and review fixes.

**Effort: Structural (downstream of Findings 1 and 3)**

### Finding 5: No New Patient Conversion Hook

I noticed that the Beacon Hill Dental homepage asks prospective patients to "Book Online" without giving them a specific reason to choose this practice over the next result. The homepage headline is "Dedicated Family Dental Care in Calgary" — clear, but not differentiated. Three of the practice's strongest assets — 35+ years of experience, eight languages spoken, and extended weekday hours until 8PM — are not featured above the fold or in the primary CTA section.

[PULL QUOTE: "The homepage answers the question 'what do you do?' It doesn't yet answer the question 'why should I choose you?'"]

**Why this matters**

[STAT CALLOUT: 0 — Visible new patient offers — The homepage has a booking CTA but no specific reason to choose this practice over a competitor]

A prospective patient who lands on the Beacon Hill Dental website has usually already passed the awareness stage — they found the practice somehow. The homepage's job is to convert that interest into a booked appointment. That conversion is harder when the page doesn't give the patient anything to grab onto beyond a generic "Book Online" button.

The practice has real differentiators. A family ownership team — Dr. Chang and his two dentist children — signals long-term commitment and genuine community investment, which matters to families making a long-term dental decision. Dr. Chang's 35+ years of career experience adds a depth of clinical credibility that newer practices can't match. Eight languages spoken is a genuine advantage for a significant portion of NW Calgary's population. Evening hours three nights a week means working patients don't have to take time off. None of these are currently featured at the decision point.

A new patient offer adds a specific reason to act now rather than later. It doesn't need to be a deep discount — even a free consultation for new patients considering implants or Invisalign is enough to differentiate the booking CTA.

**Here's what I'd do**

**Homepage headline and subhead (update with SmileShop Marketing):**
Replace the current generic subhead with something that surfaces the practice's actual differentiators. For example:

*"Three dentists. One family. Evening appointments. We speak your language."*

This tells the new patient three things immediately: the practice is family-owned with depth of commitment, it's accessible, and it may be able to serve them in their preferred language. An alternative framing that leads with clinical experience: *"35+ years of dental experience. A family practice built for NW Calgary."*

**New patient offer (coordinate with front desk — and review with ADA&C first):**
The goal is a clear, specific reason to book at this practice rather than the next result. That could be a first-visit package, a consultation for a specific service, or a welcome message for new patients to the area. The exact language matters: dental advertising in Alberta is regulated by the Alberta Dental Association & College, and the ADA&C has specific guidelines around promotional language, pricing claims, and what constitutes an inducement. Before publishing any new patient offer, the practice should review the proposed wording against the ADA&C advertising guidelines and run it past their professional association if there's any question. "New to the area? Ask about our new patient welcome package" is a safe framing. Any language that mentions pricing, free services, or complimentary appointments needs to clear that review first.

The point isn't to avoid having an offer — it's to make sure the offer you publish doesn't create a compliance problem. The underlying intent (give a new patient a reason to choose you over the next practice) is sound.

**Surface the multilingual differentiator:**
Add a simple visual element to the homepage (a flags or language icons strip, or a short line in the hero) that makes the language capability immediately visible to a patient who speaks Mandarin, Punjabi, or Tagalog. This one change can meaningfully improve conversion for a segment of the local population that is actively searching for a practice that can communicate with them.

**Effort: Medium (requires website copy updates and coordination with SmileShop Marketing)**

## Other Things I Noticed

**The blog is new, but the direction is right.** Four posts since December 2025, all targeting NW Calgary local intent. "Emergency Dentist in NW Calgary," "Dental Implant Cost in Calgary," "Finding the Right Dentist in NW Calgary" — these are exactly the right search terms to be building content around. The cadence needs to increase to 2 posts per month, and individual service pages for implants, Invisalign, and cosmetic dentistry should be built out with the same local intent focus.

**Facebook appears active.** A post was published within 24 hours of this audit. That's a good signal. The content direction could be made more strategic — a mix of patient education, team introductions (multilingual staff is a differentiator worth showing), and social proof would serve the practice better than general wellness content. The multilingual angle, in particular, could be a significant driver of new patient discovery among communities underserved by local practices.

**Instagram presence is thin.** The website links to @beacon.hill.dental, but the account's posting recency and follower count weren't confirmable at audit time. Given the Facebook activity, the Instagram likely needs attention. Even 2–3 posts per week from a mobile phone — team photos, quick patient education videos, before/after cases with permission — would begin building a presence without requiring a content production team.

**The multilingual differentiator is being left on the table.** This comes up repeatedly because it matters. Eight languages in northwest Calgary isn't a checkbox. It's a reason for a Punjabi-speaking family to drive past two other dental offices to come here. That fact should be on the homepage, in the GBP description, on social media, and eventually in targeted content. Right now it lives in the footer navigation as a language selector — functionally useful, but invisible as a marketing signal.

**RecallMax is an asset that likely has untapped capability.** The practice already uses RecallMax for online booking. RecallMax also includes automated recall, reactivation messaging, and patient communication features. Whether those features are configured and running is worth confirming with whoever manages the platform. If recall and reactivation are not actively running, turning them on costs nothing and immediately supports patient retention.
