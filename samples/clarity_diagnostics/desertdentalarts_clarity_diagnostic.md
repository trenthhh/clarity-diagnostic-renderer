# Desert Dental Arts has 43 five-star reviews. Fifteen of them are on Google

**5 findings. The fixes. In order of impact.**

Desert Dental Arts, Inc — Palm Desert, CA

## The 30-Second Read

The clinical picture at Desert Dental Arts is unusually clear: a 5.0-star Google average across every visible review, patient relationships documented at 10 and 24 years, and a hygienist — Kathy — who patients name specifically and credit as the reason they've stayed. By every measure inside the practice, the experience is what most practices spend years trying to build. The loyalty is real. This is not a practice coasting — it is a practice that has been earning trust for nearly two decades.

The problem is not the practice. The problem is the pipeline. A review solicitation platform the practice has been using — PracticeMojo — has been quietly routing satisfied patients to a third-party review site instead of Google. The result: PracticeMojo holds 43 reviews at 5.0 stars while Google holds 15. And those 15 Google reviews, combined with a Google Business Profile that has no description, three photos, and zero posts, have left Desert Dental Arts invisible on every non-branded local search. In Palm Desert, "dentist near me" surfaces competitors with 219, 500, and 765 Google reviews. Desert Dental Arts doesn't appear at all.

At 3–5 lost new patient inquiries per week — patients who searched for a Palm Desert dentist and simply never encountered this practice — and an industry-average dental patient lifetime value of $800–$1,500, the annual revenue exposure from this gap is estimated in the range of $30,000–$100,000. That's before accounting for the additional conversion loss from a website that doesn't render on mobile and a phone-only booking path that goes unanswered three days a week.

On the following pages, you'll find 5 specific findings, what each one is costing you, and what I'd do about each one.

## How I See This

When I looked at Desert Dental Arts, what struck me first wasn't what was broken. It was what was working and being wasted.

The review record is clear: patients stay 10, 15, 24 years. They mention Kathy by name. They call this the best dentist they've ever had. Google surfaces the phrases "warm atmosphere," "best dentist," and "attention to detail" as the dominant signals from what's visible. These are not template compliments. They reflect nearly two decades of consistent clinical delivery. The loyalty is real and it shows up in the language patients reach for when they describe this practice.

But here is the gap: that loyalty has never made it to Google in any volume that matters. Seventeen years of patient relationships have produced 15 Google reviews. The lowest-ranked direct competitor appearing in the Palm Desert Maps pack holds 219. Desert Dental Arts doesn't appear in that pack at all.

The reason is not that patients won't review this practice. PracticeMojo's 43 reviews prove they will. Patients are completing the feedback loop — they receive the solicitation, click the link, and leave a five-star rating. The system is working. It's just aimed at a platform that Google cannot see. PracticeMojo reviews do not factor into Maps ranking. Google reviews do. That distinction — which sounds technical — is the root cause of everything else in this diagnostic.

This is important to understand because it changes the nature of the fix. Desert Dental Arts doesn't need to convince patients to review them. That willingness already exists. What needs to change is where the link points.

Layered on top of that is a GBP listing that doesn't give Google a reason to show the practice even when a review advantage develops — no description, three photos, no posts, a single generic category. And a website that, when a new patient finds the practice through a branded search and visits on their phone, renders at full desktop width with navigation that doesn't function. And a booking path that goes unanswered Friday through Sunday.

None of these problems require reinventing the practice. The clinical foundation is solid. What's missing is the infrastructure that lets a patient searching "Palm Desert dentist" on a Tuesday evening find this practice, understand why they'd choose it, and schedule before they search again in the morning.

## The Findings

### Finding 1: The review pipeline has been aimed at the wrong target for years

PracticeMojo, the platform Desert Dental Arts uses to solicit patient feedback, shows 43 reviews at 5.0 stars. Google shows 15. No new Google reviews have appeared in the past two years. That gap — and that velocity — tells a specific story.

PracticeMojo is doing its job: it's sending patients a review request, and patients are responding. The practice has an active patient base willing to leave feedback. But PracticeMojo is routing those reviews to its own platform — not to Google. PracticeMojo reviews don't appear in Google search results. They don't factor into Maps pack ranking. For the purpose of new patient discovery in Palm Desert, those 43 reviews don't exist in the places that matter.

[INSERT SCREENSHOT: desertdentalarts_maps_city_dentist.png — Caption to use: "Maps results for 'Palm Desert dentist' — Desert Dental Arts does not appear. Competitors with 219, 500, and 765 Google reviews hold the visible positions."]

Meanwhile, the three practices appearing in the Maps pack for "Palm Desert dentist" hold 219, 500, and 765 Google reviews respectively. Desert Dental Arts' 15 don't register as a ranking signal against that field. At current velocity — effectively zero — that deficit compounds every month the PracticeMojo setting stays unchanged.

**Why this matters**

Review count is the most visible competitive signal in the Google Maps pack. Not star rating — volume. Desert Dental Arts has a 5.0 average, better than several competitors that outrank it. The difference isn't quality. It's Google-visible quantity. At an estimated 100–200 active patients, even 1–2 new Google reviews per patient per year under active solicitation would move the practice from 15 reviews toward competitive range within 18–24 months. At zero velocity, that timeline becomes infinite.

This is also the highest-leverage single intervention in the entire diagnostic. Because the patients are already willing to review, because the infrastructure already exists, the fix is a redirection — not a rebuild.

**Here's what I'd do**

First, check whether PracticeMojo allows you to edit the destination review link. Log into PracticeMojo and navigate to the review request settings. Look for the URL that patients are sent to after receiving the request. If you can change that link, replace it with the direct Google review URL for Desert Dental Arts. The Google review link follows this format: `https://search.google.com/local/writereview?placeid=[YOUR_PLACE_ID]`. The Place ID can be found by searching the practice name on Google Maps, clicking "Share," and extracting the ID from the link.

If PracticeMojo doesn't allow you to change the destination — or if the platform contract is up for renewal — the replacement workflow is simple: a post-appointment text message sent 24–48 hours after the visit, from the office number, with a direct Google review link. Most practice management platforms — Dentrix, Eaglesoft, Curve Dental — either have native messaging features or integrate with tools like Weave or Podium that make this close to automatic. The message should be personal: first name, mention of who they saw, and a plain-language ask with the direct link. No request forms, no pre-screens. A patient willing to leave five stars will leave five stars. The only thing standing between that review and Google is the link they're given.

The goal for the first 90 days is to get from effectively zero new Google reviews per month to 8–10. That's the minimum velocity needed to start accumulating Maps ranking influence.

**Effort:** Quick win if PracticeMojo allows the destination change (a settings edit); Medium if a new solicitation workflow needs to be built outside PracticeMojo (1–2 weeks to set up and activate)

### Finding 2: The GBP listing gives Google no reason to show this practice beyond "dentist in Palm Desert"

Desert Dental Arts' Google Business Profile contains: one owner headshot, one interior photo, and one auto-generated Street View image. A blank business description field. Zero Google Business Posts. A primary category of "Dentist" with no secondary service categories added.

[INSERT SCREENSHOT: desertdentalarts_gbp_listing.png — Caption to use: "Desert Dental Arts' GBP listing — blank description, three photos, no posts, and a single generic category."]

Each of these is an independent ranking signal, and each one is working against visibility on its own. Together they compound. The blank description means Google has no keyword context for this listing — the practice's city, service names, and doctor's name appear nowhere in the field Google reads to match listings to searches. "Palm Desert family dentist," "Palm Desert dental implants," and "Palm Desert cosmetic dentist" all require keyword signals that this listing doesn't provide. The three-photo count puts Desert Dental Arts at the lowest visible threshold of any practice in the competitive set; photo count and recency are documented Maps ranking factors. The absence of service-specific categories — no Cosmetic Dentist, no Family Dentist, no Implant Dentist added as secondary categories — means the algorithm has no basis to surface this listing for any search more specific than "dentist."

**Why this matters**

A GBP listing is the practice's storefront on Google. Before a prospective patient visits the website, before they read a single review, before they call — they see the GBP. A listing with no description, three photos, and no recent posts sends a signal to the algorithm and to the patient at the same moment: this practice is not actively maintaining its digital presence. For the algorithm, that means suppressed ranking. For the patient, it raises a quiet but real question: is this place still active?

The direct competitors appearing in the Maps pack for "Palm Desert dentist" appear to have 50–200+ photos, active post histories, and complete descriptions. The gap isn't hard to see.

**Here's what I'd do**

There are four actions here, in order of time required:

*GBP description (30 minutes):* Write a 750-character description that names the practice, the city, the doctor's name, and the primary service categories: family dentistry, cosmetic dentistry, dental implants, and any other services the practice actively provides. Include a neighborhood or landmark reference to support local signals ("located in Palm Desert near [landmark]"). Avoid keyword stuffing — Google reads natural prose and responds better to it. This field is largely unused by most practices and represents disproportionate ranking leverage for the time invested.

*Secondary categories (10 minutes):* In the GBP editor, navigate to Business category and add secondary categories for every service the practice actually provides. At minimum, this should include Cosmetic Dentist and Family Dentist. If dental implants are a primary service, add the implant-specific category as applicable. These categories are the mechanism by which Google indexes the listing for service-specific searches — the practice can't appear for "Palm Desert dental implants" if the GBP hasn't been told that's a service it offers.

*Photo library (ongoing):* Schedule a 1-hour practice photo session — a professional photographer or a team member with a modern smartphone — to capture the reception area, treatment rooms, team photos, and exterior. The target is 30–50 owner-uploaded photos to start, with 5–10 added monthly until the library reaches 75+. Patients want to see where they're going before they arrive. Auto-generated Street View images don't count toward the owner-upload metric that influences ranking.

*Monthly GBP posts (ongoing):* GBP posts carry local SEO weight in addition to informational value. One post per month — a service update, a care tip, a team announcement, or a seasonal note — maintains the recency signal that dormant listings lose. These don't need to be elaborate. A paragraph and a photo is enough.

**Effort:** Quick win for description and categories (under 1 hour); Medium for the photo library (requires a photo session and an ongoing upload habit)

### Finding 3: The website doesn't work on a mobile phone

Desert Dental Arts' website, built on GoDaddy Website Builder, renders at full desktop width at a 390px mobile viewport — the standard size for an iPhone. The navigation bar displays as a full horizontal row at reduced text size. No hamburger menu appears. No mobile-optimized layout is present. At the viewport size most patients are using when they search for a dentist from their phone, the site is not navigable.

[INSERT SCREENSHOT: desertdentalarts_homepage_mobile.png — Caption to use: "Desert Dental Arts' website rendered at iPhone viewport width — the full desktop layout appears with no mobile adaptation."]

Mobile Largest Contentful Paint — the performance metric Google uses to measure how quickly the main visible content loads — comes in at 3.3 seconds. Google's "Good" threshold is under 2.5 seconds. The real-world traffic data returns "Not enough data," meaning actual visitor volume is too low for Google to establish a reliable performance baseline.

**Why this matters**

The majority of local search traffic arrives on mobile. When a patient finds Desert Dental Arts — through a branded search, a referral, a directory — and taps through to the website, they land on a page built for a desktop screen that they're viewing on a phone. The navigation is small and difficult to use. Finding the office hours, services, or phone number requires pinching, zooming, and horizontal scrolling. The phone number may not be formatted as a tappable click-to-call link.

For a practice already invisible on non-branded searches, the branded search — patients who already know the name and are looking it up — represents nearly all inbound website traffic. This is the one visit that should convert. A non-functioning mobile experience at that moment is a significant conversion failure.

**Here's what I'd do**

Start by logging into the GoDaddy Website Builder and checking whether a mobile-responsive template is available for the current site design. GoDaddy's newer templates are responsive by default; the question is whether the existing site was built on an older template that predates that standard. If a responsive template is available and a switch is possible without a full rebuild, that's the first step — preview it at mobile width before committing, then audit the result on an actual phone.

If the GoDaddy builder can't produce a clean mobile experience — which is a real platform constraint with some older GoDaddy builds — this is a signal that the site needs to migrate to a proper content management system. WordPress with a managed dental hosting provider is the most common path for practices of this size. A migration takes longer and costs more than a template switch, but a non-functional mobile experience compounds its cost every day it remains: every patient who finds the practice through a branded search and cannot navigate the site is a patient who moves on.

One specific thing to verify regardless of the approach: the phone number on the site should be formatted as a clickable link so a patient can call with a single tap. On a desktop-rendered mobile layout, that link may exist in the code but be difficult to target accurately with a finger. Worth checking before anything else is changed.

**Effort:** Quick win if a responsive GoDaddy template is available and can be applied without a full rebuild; Structural if a platform migration is required (typically outside the scope of a DIY project)

### Finding 4: The practice is unreachable four days a week, with no way to book outside business hours

The Contact page on Desert Dental Arts' website states "Call us and we'll get you the answer" as the only path to scheduling an appointment. No online booking widget. No appointment request form. No live chat. No fallback for after-hours inquiry.

Office hours are Monday through Thursday, 8 AM to 5 PM. The GBP listing confirms no Friday, Saturday, or Sunday availability.

[INSERT SCREENSHOT: desertdentalarts_appointment_page.png — Caption to use: "Desert Dental Arts' contact page — a phone call is the only available action, with no online booking path present."]

**Why this matters**

A meaningful share of dental research and booking activity happens outside business hours — Friday evenings, Saturday mornings, Sunday afternoons. For a practice operating Monday through Thursday, that's not a small window. It's more than half the week.

A patient who finds Desert Dental Arts on Friday evening has no path to act. The phone goes unanswered. There is no form to submit. There is no widget to capture the intent. By Monday morning, a portion of those patients have already scheduled somewhere else. The direct competitors appearing in the Palm Desert Maps pack appear to offer booking links from their GBP listings — which means a patient who finds them after hours can schedule before the next business day arrives. Desert Dental Arts cannot compete for that patient at that moment.

The four-day schedule is an operational reality and not the diagnostic's call to change. But the absence of online booking makes the impact of a compressed week significantly worse. A practice closed on Fridays that has real-time booking is reachable 24 hours a day for patients who want to schedule. Three to five patients a week, slipping away not because they didn't want to book but because there was nothing to book into. At $800–$1,500 in patient lifetime value, the math isn't complicated.

**Here's what I'd do**

The booking friction is the more tractable problem, so that's where I'd start. NexHealth, LocalMed, and Dental Intelligence all offer real-time self-scheduling integrations that connect directly to most practice management platforms — Dentrix, Eaglesoft, and Curve Dental among them. They sync with the actual schedule in real time, so patients see only genuinely open slots and can confirm without staff involvement. Setup typically takes 1–2 weeks. Once live, the widget goes on the contact page, the homepage, and — critically — into the GBP listing as a direct "Book" button. That last step means a patient who finds Desert Dental Arts on Google can schedule without leaving the Google interface.

As an interim step, before the integration is live, I'd add a simple appointment request form to the Contact page. A form captures intent at the moment a patient is ready. A phone number that goes unanswered on a Friday night does not.

**Effort:** Medium for the real-time booking integration (1–2 weeks to configure); hours for an interim request form

### Finding 5: Zero review responses — 15 reviews, all five stars, none acknowledged

None of Desert Dental Arts' 15 Google reviews have received an owner response. The most recent visible review is approximately two years old. The oldest is six years old. The response rate is 0% across the full visible review set.

[INSERT SCREENSHOT: desertdentalarts_gbp_reviews.png — Caption to use: "Desert Dental Arts' Google reviews — every review is five stars, and none have received an owner response."]

**Why this matters**

Review responses do two things simultaneously, and Desert Dental Arts is missing both.

The first is an SEO signal. Google's local ranking algorithm treats active GBP engagement as a positive ranking input. A practice that responds to reviews is demonstrating to Google that the listing is actively managed. That engagement signal has value. A 0% response rate forfeits it entirely — especially for a listing that already needs every ranking signal it can accumulate.

The second is a trust signal, and this is the one that matters to the patient sitting in the decision seat. A prospective patient reading Desert Dental Arts' profile encounters genuinely compelling reviews: a patient describing 24 years of care, another naming Kathy specifically, another calling it the best dental experience they've had. And then: silence. No acknowledgment. The reviews describe a warm, relationship-driven practice. The response record doesn't reflect that at all.

Responding to all 15 existing reviews is a 30–60 minute effort, and it should happen before any new solicitation push goes live — because the first thing a new reviewer often checks is how previous reviewers were treated.

**Here's what I'd do**

Write personalized responses to all 15 existing Google reviews. Each response should do three things: address the reviewer by name, reference something specific from what they wrote — the staff member they mentioned, the procedure they described, how long they've been a patient — and close with a line of genuine thanks that sounds like it came from a person.

A response to a 24-year patient looks something like this: "[Name], 24 years — that means more than we can say in a reply. We're so glad you've been part of the practice for that long, and we look forward to many more. Thank you for taking the time to share this."

For future reviews, build 5–7 response templates tied to the recurring themes in Desert Dental Arts' reviews: long-term patient relationships, Kathy's care specifically, the warmth of the atmosphere, and the quality of the clinical work. Each template has structure but needs two or three personal details filled in — the reviewer's name, the staff member mentioned, one line from the review itself. That personalization takes 60–90 seconds per response and is worth every one of them. Prospective patients who haven't yet reviewed this practice are reading these responses before they decide whether to call.

**Effort:** Quick win (responding to all 15 existing reviews: one focused session of 30–60 minutes; ongoing response time is 2–3 minutes per review with templates)

## Other Things I Noticed

A few additional items came up in the diagnostic that didn't make the top five — either because the impact is lower relative to what's already listed, or because they're downstream of a higher-priority fix.

**Homepage CTA and new patient path.** The only button above the fold on the homepage is "MY CHART," linking to an existing patient portal. There is no new patient offer, no "Book an Appointment" button, and no value proposition for a first-time visitor. A prospective patient who lands from a branded search sees a call to action that signals "this site is for existing patients" before they find a reason to call. Adding a single new patient CTA above the fold — even a simple "New Patients Welcome" button linking to the contact page — closes the most immediate gap. The insurance page has a related issue: it contains generic policy text with no carrier names, no in-network designation, and no information about a membership plan for uninsured patients. These are the questions new patients most commonly arrive with, and the page doesn't answer them. Happy to share more detail on either if it's useful.

**Facebook address error and social media dormancy.** Facebook last posted on February 17, 2016. Instagram returns a 404. These are meaningful gaps in the long run, but they're the lowest-leverage starting point when the GBP and review pipeline aren't working yet — get the foundation right first. One exception worth acting on now: the Facebook business address currently lists "Hawthorne, CA 90250" rather than Palm Desert, CA. That's a data error that suppresses local discovery even within Facebook's own search. Correcting it takes two minutes in Facebook Business settings and should happen regardless of where social media falls in the priority sequence.

**No paid advertising.** Running paid search ads to the current website — non-responsive on mobile, phone-only booking, no new patient offer — would accelerate wasted spend. Paid advertising belongs at the end of this rebuild, not the front. Once the mobile experience works, booking is online, and the GBP has enough authority to compete in organic results, paid search becomes a meaningful multiplier. Before that, it amplifies the wrong experience.

**Privacy policy and HIPAA footer.** A privacy policy is absent from the site footer. This is a compliance question, not a marketing question — but it's worth a conversation with your attorney to understand the exposure. I'd flag it rather than leave it unaddressed.
