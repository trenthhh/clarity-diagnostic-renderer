## The Findings

### Finding 1: A Decade of Patients — Without a System to Turn Them Into Proof

I noticed that Peak Dental Group has 131 Google reviews after approximately 10 years in operation. The 4.7-star rating tells me the patient experience is strong — people who visit clearly like what they find. The three most recent reviews I saw were from January and February 2026, pointing to a current velocity of roughly 2–3 new reviews per month.

[GRAPHIC: competitive_ranking — Beacon Smiles Dental: 2884, Sarcee Dental: 1087, Nolan Hill Dental: 1061, Peak Dental Group: 131 — Highlight: Peak Dental Group — Caption: "Google review counts, NW Calgary dental practices, April 2026"]

**Why this matters**

[STAT CALLOUT: 26% — Of the 500-review competitive benchmark for a 10-year practice — 131 reviews accumulated over a decade, without a solicitation system in place]

The review gap between Peak Dental and its nearest major competitors isn't a patient satisfaction problem — the 4.7-star rating makes that clear. It's the gap that accumulates when a practice delivers excellent care without any mechanism to convert that experience into digital proof. Nolan Hill Dental, two kilometers from the Nolanridge location, has 1,061 Google reviews. Sarcee Dental has 1,087. Beacon Smiles Dental — the dominant NW Calgary practice by review authority — has 2,884. These practices are not better at dentistry. They have systems that turn every appointment into a visible signal.

Review volume, velocity, and recency are among the signals that influence local Maps ranking, and in competitive markets they often become major differentiators. A practice at 131 reviews accumulating at 2–3 per month will not appear organically in the Maps pack for competitive NW Calgary searches — the research confirms this across all five service terms. For a practice offering dental implants and full-arch implant treatment, Maps invisibility carries a direct financial cost. Patients searching "Calgary NW dental implants" are high-intent and ready to book. Using a rough implant case value range of $5,000–$15,000 per case and a full-arch case value range of $15,000–$35,000 per case, even one missed case per month could represent $60,000–$420,000 in annual production opportunity — a range that reflects how much the mix matters. The mechanism is the same regardless of where the actual number lands: high-intent searches, high case values, and the practice isn't there.

[PULL QUOTE: "The patients are happy. The system just never asked them to say so."]

**Here's what I'd do**

**Build the solicitation pipeline (weeks 1–2):**
Most practice management systems support post-appointment automation. Set up an SMS or email that sends 2–4 hours after appointment completion — not immediately, not the next morning — with a single sentence and a direct link to the Google review form. Short, low friction: "Thanks for coming in today — if you have a moment, we'd love a Google review: [link]." That's the whole message.

**Train the front desk on a verbal ask (same week):**
The automation handles volume. The in-person ask handles patients who prefer it. One sentence at checkout: "We really appreciate Google reviews — if you were happy today, it helps other families find us." No script pressure, just a natural ask.

**Respond to every review (ongoing):**
Owner responses may support the engagement and trust signals that matter both to prospective patients and to Google's local ranking assessment. Personalized responses — something specific from the review rather than a generic thank-you — signal that someone at the practice is paying attention. Aim to respond within 48 hours of every new review.

**Effort:** Structural

### Finding 2: The Facebook Page Has Told Patients the Clinic Is Closed for Six Years

I noticed that Peak Dental Group's Facebook page is currently listed as "Permanently Closed." Based on available evidence, this designation appears to have been in place since approximately 2020 — though I can't confirm the exact date from what I had access to. The practice has operated 7 days a week continuously for 10 years. The Facebook page has 11 followers.

[PULL QUOTE: "The practice is open every day of the week. Facebook says it shut down when the world went into lockdown — and has never been corrected."]

**Why this matters**

[STAT CALLOUT: ~6 years — Approximate duration of the "Permanently Closed" Facebook status — While the practice operated 7 days a week throughout]

Every patient who receives a referral and checks Facebook before calling is told the practice no longer exists. Every patient who clicks the paid Maps ad, then verifies on Facebook — same result. Every existing patient who mentions Peak Dental to a friend, who then looks it up — same result. Referral chains break at this touchpoint invisibly, with no signal back to the practice that it's happening.

This isn't a missed engagement opportunity. It's active misdirection that has been running for approximately six years. The volume of lost patient opportunities isn't measurable, but the mechanism is clear: anyone who touches this listing before calling is told not to bother.

**Here's what I'd do**

This is the fastest fix in the audit. Here's the exact sequence:

Log into **Facebook Business Manager** → your Page → **Page Settings** → **Page Info** → find the operating status field → change from "Permanently Closed" to **"Open"** → update hours to match the current GBP listing (Monday–Friday with evening hours to 7 PM, Saturday and Sunday 9 AM–5 PM) → add a current profile photo (team photo or clinic exterior) → add a current cover image → confirm the address at 155 Nolanridge Ct NW #135 is accurate → save.

Total time: **under 30 minutes. This can be done today, before any other fix in this audit.**

The page doesn't need to become a content hub immediately. The minimum viable state is accurate status, accurate hours, a current photo so it doesn't look abandoned. Content strategy can follow. The status fix is urgent.

**Effort:** Quick win

### Finding 3: Google Can't Place Peak Dental for Implants, Invisalign, or Cosmetic Searches — Because the GBP Doesn't Know They Offer Them

I noticed that Peak Dental Group's GBP is categorized as "Dentist" — the generic default, with no service-specific secondary categories confirmed. I also noticed that Aesthetic Dental Studio NW Calgary, a competitor with 83 Google reviews, ranks organically for "Calgary NW cosmetic dentist." Peak Dental has 131 reviews, offers cosmetic services, and does not appear in the same search.

[GRAPHIC: comparison_stat — "Aesthetic Dental Studio NW Calgary: 83 reviews — ranks for 'Calgary NW cosmetic dentist'" vs "Peak Dental Group: 131 reviews — does not appear in that search" — Caption: "The difference isn't review count. Aesthetic Dental has a Cosmetic Dentist GBP category. Peak Dental has 'Dentist.'"]

**Why this matters**

[STAT CALLOUT: 4 of 5 — Organic Maps appearances for Nosehill Dental Centre (391 reviews) — Peak Dental (4.7★, 131 reviews) appears in 0 of the same 5 searches]

Nosehill Dental Centre — 4.6 stars, 391 reviews — appears organically in four of the five highest-value NW Calgary service searches. Peak Dental — 4.7 stars, 131 reviews — appears in none. The difference isn't star rating. It isn't primarily review count, either: Nosehill doesn't hold a meaningful review advantage over Peak Dental in any category that would explain the ranking gap alone. GBP configuration is one of the primary signals Google uses when matching businesses to local service queries. A practice listed only as "Dentist" will not reliably surface for "cosmetic dentist" or "dental implants" searches — there's no category signal to match against. Adding service-specific secondary categories gives Google what it needs to include this practice in the results that matter most. Category changes often produce measurable Maps movement within 30 days, typically ahead of any review velocity improvement.

The practice's strongest differentiator — Dr. Gill's national and global recognition — also appears nowhere in the GBP description or listing. That credential, placed in the first line of the GBP description, gives any patient who finds the listing a reason to choose this practice before they ever click through to the website.

[PULL QUOTE: "Aesthetic Dental Studio has 83 reviews and ranks for cosmetic searches. Peak Dental has 131 and doesn't appear. That gap isn't about reviews."]

**Here's what I'd do**

**GBP categories (30 minutes):**
Log into the GBP dashboard → Edit Profile → Business Category. Keep "Dentist" as primary. Add secondary categories including Cosmetic Dentist, Dental Implants Provider, Emergency Dental Service, and Pediatric Dentist. If the Invisalign Provider category is available in Alberta, add it — if not, reinforce Invisalign through your services list, GBP description, and posts instead. Each secondary category expands the range of service searches the practice can appear for.

**GBP description (1–2 hours):**
Write a 750-character description that leads with the practice's differentiators. A strong opening: "Peak Dental Group is an award-winning NW Calgary dental clinic led by Dr. Sameet Gill, DMD MBA — named Dentist of the Year in Canada and Young Dentist of the Year globally at the 2024 Forttuna Global Excellence Awards. We provide dental implants, full-arch implant treatment, Invisalign, cosmetic dentistry, and full-family care — 7 days a week, with evening hours Monday and Friday." Pack in service keywords naturally. Google treats this description as part of its relevance assessment for local queries.

**GBP posts (monthly):**
The April 22 post about Denture Implants is a good start — keep the cadence going. The next post should feature Dr. Gill's award: a photo, a brief description of the recognition, a link to book. One post per month is the floor. Two to three is better.

**Effort:** Medium

### Finding 4: The Practice Is Open 7 Days a Week — But Can't Capture a Patient Who Finds Them at 8 PM Saturday

I noticed that Peak Dental Group's website offers a "Request Appointment" button that routes to a contact form — not a real-time scheduling system. No self-scheduling platform was found on the site. The practice operates Monday through Sunday, with Monday and Friday evening hours to 7 PM.

[GRAPHIC: comparison_stat — "Physical access: 7 days a week, including evenings" vs "Online self-booking: 0 hours available" — Caption: "Peak Dental has built the most accessible hours schedule in NW Calgary — without the booking infrastructure to capture patients who discover the practice outside staffed hours."]

**Why this matters**

Most NW Calgary dental practices are closed Sundays. Many close by 5 PM on weekdays. Peak Dental's 7-day schedule with extended evening hours is a genuine competitive advantage — the kind of access that working families and patients with dental emergencies specifically search for. It should be a central marketing message. Right now, it's an operational asset that can't be acted on after hours.

[PULL QUOTE: "Peak Dental built physical access that beats every nearby competitor — with no way for a patient to book into it after business hours."]

A patient who discovers the practice at 8 PM Saturday — through a Google search, a referral, or a paid ad click — cannot self-schedule. They submit a form and wait for a callback the next business day. If they're searching Saturday evening, they're comparing options. Any competitor with a live booking button gets the appointment.

The paid Maps ad sharpens this further. A patient clicks the ad, lands on a contact form instead of a booking flow, and may then check Facebook (Finding 2). The ad spend is real. The conversion infrastructure doesn't match it.

[SIDEBAR: "A note on the Emergency Exam offer: The $77 Emergency Exam is the kind of accessible entry point that can be effective for urgent-care patient acquisition. Before publishing this price point in paid advertising, it's worth reviewing the language against the Alberta Dental Association & College (ADA&C) advertising guidelines, which regulate pricing claims and promotional offers in Alberta. The underlying intent — giving a new patient a clear, compelling reason to choose this practice for emergency care — is sound strategy. The specific framing should be confirmed against current ADA&C requirements before going live."]

**Here's what I'd do**

**Select a booking platform (week 1):**
Jane App is a well-known Canadian online booking option used across various healthcare practices, but the right platform depends on your current practice management system's integration capabilities. Other options include NexHealth, Dentrix Online Booking, and the native booking tools available through your existing software. Evaluate based on integration fit, not brand familiarity.

**Configure and launch (weeks 2–4):**
Set up appointment types, available time slots, and provider availability. Build in a brief intake form so the team has context before each new patient appointment. Test the full booking flow on a mobile phone — most after-hours patients book on phones. The experience should be fast and require as few taps as possible.

**Update every touchpoint:**
Replace the "Request Appointment" contact form links across the site with the live booking URL. Add the booking link to the GBP profile — there's a direct booking link field in the GBP dashboard. Add it to the Instagram bio and the corrected Facebook page per Finding 2. The goal is one click from any discovery channel to a booked appointment, regardless of the time.

**Effort:** Medium

## Other Things I Noticed

**The same-complex competitor is smaller — for now.** Nolan Ridge Dental operates at 255 Nolanridge Ct NW — the same complex as Peak Dental. They currently have 113 Google reviews to Peak Dental's 131, with a comparable 4.7-star rating. Peak Dental holds the review advantage in its own building right now. That advantage erodes if Nolan Ridge implements a solicitation system before Peak Dental does. Worth tracking monthly.

**Paid Maps ads without an organic foundation have a cost ceiling.** The Emergency Exam campaign is a reasonable short-term tactic. But paid visibility is rented — it disappears when the budget does. The organic systems described in Findings 1 and 3 build compounding authority that increases over time and eventually makes the ad spend optional rather than structural. The path is to build toward that transition, not to depend on paid ads indefinitely.

**The 4.7-star rating is an asset, not a liability.** In a competitive Maps pack where most practices cluster between 4.8 and 4.9, the gap is small enough that GBP optimization and review volume will carry more weight than star rating alone. As reviews accumulate at higher velocity, the rating may improve naturally. Responding to every review — particularly any critical ones — demonstrates active engagement that prospective patients notice.

**Women-owned and LGBTQ+ friendly designations are in the GBP but absent from the website.** These attributes appear in the GBP listing, where some patients will see them. They belong on the website too — briefly and matter-of-factly, on the homepage or About page. Some patients specifically filter for practices with these designations, and a website mention makes the signal findable regardless of how the patient arrives.
